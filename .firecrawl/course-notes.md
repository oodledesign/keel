Search any local industry on Google and you'll find the same pattern: generic stock photos, dated typography, clashing colors, no hierarchy, layouts that scream "template."

- Typography that's been chosen, not defaulted to
- A color story — two or three colors that work together with purpose
- Real whitespace. Elements that aren't competing for attention
- Photography that feels human, not stock
- A clear hierarchy — you know where to look first

When a site has this, you feel trust before you read a single word.

Find three references that answer that question. Those become your visual standard for the project. Everything you build gets measured against them.

## The Problem With Winging It

Client gives you a brief. You open Claude. You start prompting. You get something. Client doesn't love it. You iterate. And iterate. And iterate.

Why? Because you didn't have the content. You didn't have the structure. You were designing a container before you knew what was going inside it.

Real projects don't work that way.

## Step 1 — The Sitemap

Every project starts with a sitemap. Before any design, before any section, before any prompt — you map out every page the website needs.

Home. About. Services. Contact. Maybe a blog. Maybe a portfolio. Whatever the client needs.

This is a five minute conversation with your client. What pages do you need? Write them down. Now you have your map.

## Step 2 — Page Structure

Once you know the pages, you define the sections inside each one.

For a clinic project: Hero. Why Patients Choose Us. Meet the Doctors. Services. Testimonials. Final CTA. Footer.

That's the structure — written down before opening any tool, before touching any template, before writing a single prompt. Do this for every page. 30 minutes maximum. It saves you hours of back and forth later.

## Step 3 — Client Content

Now you fill each section with real content from the client.

The hero already has a headline, subheadline, and CTA buttons. The doctors section has real names, real specialties, real bios. The testimonials are real quotes from real patients.

Every section has what it needs. AI didn't guess any of this — the client provided it and you organized it. When you open Claude, it has everything. No placeholders. No generic content. No iterating in circles.

That's the difference between a professional process and winging it.

## Step 4 — Now You Design

Now — and only now — you open the tool.

You go into the template, select the sections that match your structure, and you're ready to start. You didn't design anything yet. You just selected what you needed based on a plan you already built.

When you bring this to Claude — it has the content, the structure, and the direction. That's how fast this gets when you plan first.

You don't need to be the best designer in the world to deliver great work. You need a process.

Typography is probably the most underestimated decision you'll make on a website. Most people treat it like a finishing touch. It's not. It's one of the first things that communicates who your client is — before anyone reads a single word.

### The Rule

Most designers add too many fonts. Two, three, four — and suddenly the website feels chaotic and unprofessional.

**One font maximum.** If you really need a combination — maybe a serif for headlines and a sans-serif for body text — that's the limit. Never more than two. And honestly, most of the time one is enough.

### How to Choose

Start with the feeling you want to communicate.

- **Modern and technical** → clean sans-serif. Geometric, minimal, precise.
- **Premium and elegant** → serif. Editorial, luxury, timeless.
- **Warm and approachable** → humanist sans-serif. Friendly, accessible, trustworthy.

Go to Google Fonts. Pick one font that matches the feeling of your visual system. Type something in the preview. Does it feel right? If yes — that's your typography. Write it down.

# **Lesson Recap: Building a Color System for Websites**

Color is one of the most powerful tools in web design because it shapes a user's first impression before they read a single word. The goal isn't to choose colors you personally like—it's to create a system where every color has a clear purpose.

## The 4 Roles of Color

A successful website color palette is built around four distinct roles:

### 1\. Canvas

The canvas is the foundation of the design, typically made up of neutral colors such as white, off-white, or light gray. Its job is to create space, improve readability, and allow other colors to stand out.

### 2\. Atmosphere

Atmosphere colors create the emotional tone of the website. These are often subtle background colors, gradients, or section dividers that communicate the personality of the brand. In healthcare, for example, soft blues help create a sense of calm, cleanliness, and trust.

### 3\. Accent

Accent colors are used intentionally to attract attention. Buttons, icons, highlights, and important actions should use the accent color to guide users through the page and establish visual hierarchy.

### 4\. Contrast

Contrast colors provide structure and readability. Dark text, headings, and key interface elements rely on strong contrast to remain clear and accessible across the design.

**Remember:** Every color should have a job. When colors serve specific roles, the design becomes more intentional and easier to understand.

## How to Choose Colors

Great color palettes rarely come from random inspiration.

Instead:

1. Collect references from websites, apps, and brands within the same industry.
2. Build a moodboard and identify recurring patterns.
3. Observe which color combinations consistently communicate the right feeling.
4. Choose colors based on what fits the project, not personal preference.

The goal is to develop visual judgment by recognizing what works repeatedly in successful designs.

## A Common Mistake: Prioritizing Style Over Function

One of the easiest mistakes to make is choosing color combinations that look interesting but fail to support the design.

For example, combining strong yellow and blue backgrounds in the same gradient may seem visually appealing at first, but it can create several problems:

- Visual competition between colors
- Reduced readability
- Poor separation between elements
- Difficulty maintaining hierarchy

In the clinic design example, images placed on top of the yellow section lost contrast and blended into the background, making the content harder to understand.

A color choice is only successful if it supports the function of the design.

## Why Contrast Matters

Contrast is one of the most important principles in web design because it directly affects usability.

Strong contrast helps:

- Create visual hierarchy
- Separate sections and components
- Improve readability
- Guide attention toward important actions
- Make content easier to process

When contrast is weak, users must work harder to understand the page. The design becomes confusing instead of communicative.

## Key Takeaways

- Every color on a website should serve a specific role.
- Build palettes from references and patterns, not personal preference.
- Use a system of Canvas, Atmosphere, Accent, and Contrast colors.
- Evaluate color choices based on function, not aesthetics alone.
- Prioritize contrast to maintain hierarchy, readability, and clarity.
- If a color decision reduces usability, it is the wrong decision—even if it looks attractive.

The best color systems are not the most creative. They are the ones that communicate clearly, guide attention effectively, and support the goals of the website.

These images show the service experience in action, such as doctors consulting patients or staff interacting with visitors. Their purpose is to communicate what it feels like to engage with the business.

### Team Photography

These are portraits of the people behind the service. Team photos build trust by helping visitors connect with real people rather than a brand alone.

Before generating any images, build a moodboard.

Collect references that capture the feeling you want to communicate. For team photos, focus on lighting, expressions, and professionalism. For contextual images, focus on emotions, interactions, and atmosphere.

The goal is to establish a clear visual direction before using any AI tool.

## Direction Over Tools

Whether you use ChatGPT, Gemini, Higgsfield, or another AI platform, the process remains the same.

The quality of your results depends more on your references and creative direction than on the tool itself. Strong references consistently produce better outcomes than complicated prompts.

For team photography, consistency is essential. Similar lighting, backgrounds, and styling across all portraits create a professional and cohesive appearance.

For contextual photography, focus on the feeling you want to communicate. In the clinic project, the objective was to convey trust, care, and professionalism through warm interactions and welcoming environments.

## Start with AI

Before making any manual adjustments, ask your AI tool to make the website responsive.

In most cases, the AI will successfully:

- Adjust layouts for smaller screens
- Stack elements correctly
- Improve spacing and alignment
- Adapt sections for mobile devices

This can save hours of manual work and dramatically speed up the design process.

## When Manual Adjustments Are Needed

Occasionally, a section may not look right on mobile.

When this happens, create a simple visual reference showing how you want the section to look. A quick draft in Figma is usually enough. You do not need a polished design—just enough information for the AI to understand the desired structure.

You can also use existing mobile designs or templates as references. Providing a screenshot often helps the AI produce a much more accurate result.

## Use References to Guide the AI

Just like with color, typography, and imagery, references are one of the most effective ways to communicate your intentions.

Instead of repeatedly describing changes, show the AI what you want. A simple screenshot or wireframe can often solve a problem faster than a long prompt.

## Key Takeaways

- Always let AI handle responsiveness first.
- Most mobile layouts can be generated with a single prompt.
- Use Figma drafts when a section needs manual adjustments.
- Reference existing mobile designs whenever possible.
- Visual references help AI understand your intentions more clearly than text alone.
- Responsive design is no longer a separate phase, it is part of an efficient AI-assisted workflow.

## Two Workflows You Can Use

Throughout this module, we discussed two different approaches to working with AI.

### The Complete Workflow

This approach starts in Figma.

You design the entire website, establish all visual decisions, and then use AI to build the final product. This gives you the highest level of control and typically produces the strongest results.

### The Mixed Workflow

This approach starts with a smaller foundation.

You might begin with a few sections designed in Figma, existing templates, or components from a prompt library. From there, you collaborate with AI to expand and refine the website.

This workflow is often faster while still maintaining strong creative direction.

Both approaches are valid. The right choice depends on your project, timeline, and level of control required.

## The Most Important Principle

Regardless of which workflow you choose, never rely solely on text prompts.

AI performs best when it receives visual direction.

Use:

- Moodboards
- References
- Wireframes
- Figma designs
- Existing templates
- Screenshots

The more visual context you provide, the better the output will be. Generic prompts create generic websites. Clear direction creates unique and intentional designs.
